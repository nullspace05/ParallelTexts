I. Afternoon Class


"So — does anyone know what this pale, hazy white thing really is? People call it a river, or say it's where milk once flowed."

The teacher traced a finger down the large black star chart on the board, along the milky band blurred white from top to bottom, and turned to the class.


Campanella raised his hand. Then four or five others did the same.

Giovanni was about to raise his too, but caught himself and stopped. He was certain they were all stars — he'd read that once in a magazine — yet lately Giovanni felt half-asleep even in the classroom every day. He had no time to read, nothing to read anyway, and everything seemed to slip just beyond his grasp.


But the teacher had already noticed.


"Giovanni. You must know, don't you?"


Giovanni stood up eagerly, but once on his feet he found he couldn't answer clearly. Zanelli turned from the seat in front and giggled at him. Giovanni flushed and grew terribly flustered.

The teacher spoke again.

"When you study the Milky Way carefully through a large telescope, what is it mostly made of?"


Stars, Giovanni thought again — but once more he couldn't answer right away.


The teacher looked troubled for a moment, then turned his eyes toward Campanella.

"Then Campanella."

But Campanella, who had raised his hand so boldly, now stood squirming and still could not answer.


The teacher stared at Campanella in surprise for a while, then hurriedly said, "Well then. All right," and pointed to the star chart himself.

"When you look at this faint white Milky Way through a large telescope, it appears as countless tiny stars. Giovanni, isn't that so?"


Giovanni flushed and nodded. But before long his eyes were brimming with tears.

Yes — he had known. Of course Campanella knew too. They had read it together once in a magazine at Campanella's father's study. Campanella would even fetch a huge book from his father's bookshelf whenever they read that article, open to the page on the galaxy, and the two of them would pore forever over the beautiful photograph — a black page scattered with white dots.

Campanella couldn't have forgotten. He must have held back on purpose, pitying Giovanni — because lately work wearied him morning and afternoon, he no longer played freely with anyone at school, and he hardly spoke with Campanella at all. The thought was unbearable; Giovanni felt both he and Campanella were pitiful creatures.


The teacher spoke again.

"So if you think of this Milky Way as a real river, then each one of those little stars is like a grain of sand or pebble on the riverbed. And if you think of it as a great stream of milk, it resembles the Milky Way even more closely — each star is like a tiny globule of fat floating finely in the milk. Then you may ask what takes the place of the river's water. That is what we call a vacuum — something that carries light at a certain speed — and the sun and the earth float in it too. In other words, we ourselves live in the water of the Milky Way. And when you look around from within that water, just as deep water looks bluer, the farther and deeper you look toward the bottom of the Milky Way, the more stars seem to gather — which is why it looks white and hazy. Look at this model."


The teacher pointed to a large double-convex lens filled with many glowing grains of sand.

"The Milky Way is shaped exactly like this. We suppose that each of these glowing grains is a star shining by itself, just as our sun does. Imagine our sun is about here, and the earth lies very near it. Suppose you stand in the middle at night and look all around inside this lens. On this side the glass is thin, so you would see only a few glowing grains — that is, stars. On this side and this side the glass is thick, so you see many glowing grains — many stars — and the distant ones look faint and white. That, in short, is today's explanation of the galaxy. As for how large this lens is, and about the various stars within it — we are out of time, so I will tell you in the next science lesson. Today is the festival of the Milky Way, so everyone go outside and look well at the sky. That is all for today. Put away your books and notebooks."


For a while the whole classroom filled with the sounds of desk lids opening and closing, books being stacked — then everyone stood neatly, bowed, and filed out.



II. The Print Shop


When Giovanni left the school gate, seven or eight classmates from his section had gathered around Campanella by the cherry tree in the corner of the schoolyard — not heading home.

They seemed to be planning a trip to fetch gourds, rig them with blue lanterns, and float them down the river for tonight's star festival.


But Giovanni waved his hand broadly and strode out through the school gate.

All through the town, households were getting ready for tonight's Milky Way festival — hanging balls of holly leaves, fastening lanterns to hinoki branches, and the like.


Without going home, Giovanni turned three corners and entered a large print shop. He bowed to the plump man in a white shirt at the entrance desk, took off his shoes, and went upstairs.

He opened the large door straight ahead.

Inside, though it was still broad daylight, the electric lights blazed. Rotary presses clattered in rows. Workers with cloths tied around their heads, or lampshades drawn over their lamps, sang their lines aloud as they read and counted, busy at their tasks.


Giovanni went straight to the third high desk from the entrance and bowed. The man searched the shelves for a while, then said,

"Think you can pick up this much?" — and handed him a slip of paper.


Giovanni took a small flat case from under the man's desk and squatted in the corner by the wall, where electric lamps stood in ranks on a shelf. With a tiny pair of tweezers he began picking up type, one piece after another — each no bigger than a millet seed.

A man in a blue apron passed behind him.

"Well, well, Bug-eyes — up early."

Four or five workers nearby laughed coldly, without a sound and without turning around.


Giovanni wiped his eyes again and again as he gradually gathered the type.


After six o'clock had struck and some time had passed, Giovanni compared the flat box, now full of picked type, with the slip of paper once more, then brought it to the man at the desk. The man silently accepted it and nodded faintly.


Giovanni bowed, opened the door, and went back to the calculation desk. The man in white, as before, said nothing — only handed him a small silver coin.

Giovanni's face suddenly brightened. He bowed briskly, grabbed the bag he'd left under the desk, and burst outside.

Whistling cheerfully, he stopped at the bakery for a loaf of bread and a bag of lump sugar — then dashed off at full speed.



III. Home


Giovanni came home at a brisk pace to a small house on a back street. At the leftmost of three adjoining entrances, purple kale and asparagus grew in empty crates, and the two small windows still had their sunshades drawn down.


"Mother. I'm home. Work wasn't too bad," Giovanni said, taking off his shoes.

"Oh, Giovanni — your work must have been hard. It's cool today. I've been feeling fine all along."


Giovanni stepped up into the entryway. His mother was lying in the room just inside, a white cloth bound around her head. He opened the window.

"Mother. I bought lump sugar today. I thought I'd put some in your milk."

"Oh, you go on up first. I don't want any yet."

"Mother. When did sister get back?"

"Oh, around three. Everyone helped out around the house."

"Mother's milk hasn't come yet, has it?"

"I don't suppose it has."

"I'll go fetch it."

"Oh, there's no hurry — you go on up first. Your sister made something with tomatoes and left it over there."

"Then I'll eat."


Giovanni took the plate of tomatoes from by the window and munched for a while, bread in hand.


"Say, Mother — I think Father will surely be back before long."

"Oh, I think so too. But why do you think that?"

"Because this morning's newspaper said the fishing up north was tremendously good this year."

"Oh, but you know — Father might not have gone out fishing at all."

"He surely did. Father wouldn't have done anything bad enough to land him in prison. Those big crab shells and narwhal horns he brought back and donated to the school are still in the specimen room. Sixth-graders haul them to the classroom whenever the teacher changes for a lesson. On the school trip two years a—"


"Father said he'd bring you a sea-otter coat next time, didn't he."

"Everyone whispers that to me. They say it like they're teasing."

"They're saying mean things to you."

"Yeah — but Campanella never does. When everyone says things like that, Campanella looks sorry for me."

"They say Campanella's father and our father were childhood friends — just like you two, from when they were small."

"Oh, that's why Father took me along when he visited Campanella's house too. Those were good times. On my way home from school I'd often stop by. Campanella's house had a train that ran on an alcohol lamp. Fit seven rails together and they made a circle, complete with telegraph poles and signal markers — the lights turned blue only when the train passed."

"Once the alcohol ran out we tried kerosene instead. The can ended up completely black with soot."

"Is that so."

"I still go around delivering newspapers every morning. But the house is always dead quiet."

"Because it's so early, you see."

"There's a dog named Sauel. His tail is just like a broom. When I go he whines and follows me — all the way to the corner of town, sometimes even farther. Tonight everyone's going to float gourd lanterns down the river. The dog will surely tag along."

"That's right. Tonight is the Milky Way festival."

"Yeah. I'll watch the river while I go fetch the milk."

"Oh, go on then. Just don't go into the river."

"Yeah — I'll only watch from the bank. I'll be back in an hour."

"Stay out and play a while longer. If you're with Campanella, I won't worry."

"Yeah, we'll definitely be together. Mother — shall I close the window?"

"Oh, please do. It's cool enough now."


Giovanni stood, closed the window, and cleared away the plate and bread bag. He laced his shoes briskly.

"I'll be back in an hour and a half," he said — and stepped out through the dark doorway.



IV. Night of the Centaur Festival


Giovanni came down the hill through the town, past rows of hinoki trees turned black in the dusk. His face wore a lonely look — as though he were whistling to no one.


At the foot of the hill a single large streetlamp stood blazing, pale and splendid. The farther Giovanni descended toward it, the sharper his shadow grew. It had trailed behind him long and vague as some kind of monster; now it darkened, took shape, lifted a foot, waved a hand, and swung around to his side.

(I'm a fine locomotive. This is a grade — so I'm fast. I'm passing that lamp right now. There — now my shadow's a compass. It spun clean around and came out in front.)

So Giovanni was thinking as he strode beneath the streetlamp — when suddenly Zanelli, from school that day, stepped out of a dark alley on the far side of the light. He wore a new shirt with a pointed collar and brushed past Giovanni in a flash.

"Zanelli — you going to float gourds?" Before Giovanni could finish—

"Giovanni! A sea-otter coat's coming from your father!" the boy shouted from behind, as if hurling the words at him.


Giovanni's chest went tight. The whole world seemed to ring.


"What do you mean, Zanelli!" Giovanni shouted back — but Zanelli had already vanished into a house across the way, its lawn dimly visible.

"Why does Zanelli say things like that when I haven't done a thing? He runs like a rat. He says things like that because Zanelli's an idiot."


Giovanni walked on through streets dressed up with lamps and tree branches. His mind raced.

The clock shop blazed with neon. Every second the red eyes of a stone-carved horned owl revolved. Jewels of every kind rode a thick glass disc colored like the sea, circling slowly as stars. From the far side a copper horseman turned slowly toward the viewer. At the center stood a round black star-finder, trimmed with blue asparagus fronds.


Giovanni forgot himself, staring at the chart.

It was far smaller than the one at school — yet turn the dial to the right date and hour, and the sky at that moment would wheel through the ellipse. Down the middle ran the Milky Way, a hazy band from top to bottom; toward the bottom it seemed to fizz faintly, as if giving off steam. Behind it stood a small telescope on three legs, gleaming yellow. On the rearmost wall hung a great chart of the zodiac — constellations drawn as strange beasts, snakes, fish, jars.

Were scorpions and warriors really packed so thick in the sky? Ah — he wanted to walk through them forever. He stood there dazed a long while.


Then he remembered his mother's milk and tore himself away. He was conscious of his shabby coat at the shoulders — yet he puffed out his chest anyway, swung his arms wide, and marched through the town.


The air was perfectly clear, flowing through streets and shopfronts like water. Every streetlamp was wrapped in fresh green maple and oak boughs. Before the electric company, six plane trees glittered with strings of tiny bulbs — the whole scene like some city of mermaids.

Children in crisp new kimonos whistled the star-procession tune, shouting,

"Centaurus — scatter the dew!"

as they ran, lighting blue magnesium fireworks, playing with abandon.

But Giovanni hung his head again and hurried toward the milk shop, his thoughts somewhere far from all this noise.


Before he knew it he had reached the town's edge, where poplar trees rose one after another, high against the stars. He passed through the milk shop's black gate and stood before the dim kitchen, smelling of cattle. He removed his hat.

"Good evening," he said — but the house was silent. No one seemed to be home.

"Good evening — excuse me," Giovanni called again, standing straight. After a while an elderly woman emerged slowly, as if unwell, and muttered something about what he wanted.

"Um — the milk didn't come to our house today, so I came to fetch it," Giovanni said, putting every ounce of force into it.

"There's no one here right now. I couldn't say. Please come back tomorrow."

The woman rubbed beneath her red-rimmed eyes and looked down at him.

"My mother's ill — we need it tonight."

"Then come back a little later." She seemed ready to withdraw.


"I see. Well — thank you very much." Giovanni bowed and stepped out of the kitchen.


He was about to turn at the town crossroads when, outside the general store on the road to the bridge, he saw six or seven figures — dark shadows and pale shirts mingling. They whistled and laughed, each carrying a gourd lantern. Every voice was familiar. They were his classmates.

Giovanni nearly turned back on instinct — then thought better of it and walked toward them boldly.

"Going to the river—" He started to speak, but his throat caught.

"Giovanni! A sea-otter coat's coming!" Zanelli shouted again.

"Giovanni! A sea-otter coat's coming!" The rest joined in at once.


Giovanni flushed scarlet. He hardly knew whether he was still walking. He tried to hurry past — and there among them was Campanella.

Campanella looked on with pity, silent, smiling slightly — watching Giovanni as if to ask, You won't be angry, will you?


Giovanni dodged his eyes as if fleeing. Campanella's tall figure passed. Soon everyone was whistling at random. When Giovanni turned the corner he looked back — Zanelli was looking back too. Campanella, whistling high and clear, was already walking vaguely toward the bridge.

Giovanni grew so lonely he could not say how. He broke into a run.

Small children with hands over their ears, hopping on one foot and shrieking waaa — seeing him charge, thought it a game and squealed waaai! in delight.

Soon Giovanni was hurrying toward the black hill.



V. The Weather-Vane Pillar


Behind the pasture the land rose in a gentle hill. Its flat black summit hung vaguely lower than usual beneath the Great Bear in the north.


Giovanni climbed swiftly up a narrow path through a small grove already touched with dew. Between dark grass and dense thickets — bushes that took on all manner of shapes — the little track showed as a single white thread under the starlight.

Tiny insects in the grass gave off a sparkling blue glow. Here and there a leaf shone through, lit from within. Giovanni thought they looked like the gourd lanterns the others had carried.


He passed through a grove of pine and oak, black as ink — and suddenly the sky opened wide. The Milky Way glittered from south to north, and at the summit he could make out the weather-vane pillar.

Bellflowers — or perhaps wild chrysanthemums — bloomed across the hillside as if their scent had drifted out of a dream. A single bird crossed the hilltop, crying without pause.


Giovanni reached the weather-vane pillar at the summit and dropped his hot, heavy body onto the cold grass.


Below, the town lights glowed in the dark like a shrine at the bottom of the sea. Children's songs, whistles, and broken shouts reached him faintly. Wind sounded far off; the hill grass rustled quietly. His sweat-soaked shirt turned cold against his skin.

Giovanni gazed out over the black plain stretching far beyond the town's edge.


From somewhere came the sound of a train. Its small windows showed in a row, red and low. Giovanni pictured the travelers inside — peeling apples, laughing in every manner — and a sadness he could not name rose in him. He lifted his eyes to the sky again.


Ah — that white band across the heavens. They're all stars, they say.


But no matter how he looked, the sky did not seem the vast, cold emptiness his teacher had described at school. The longer he watched, the more it felt like a meadow scattered with groves and pastures.

He watched the blue stars of Lyra multiply to three, then four — twinkling, legs extending and withdrawing again and again, stretching long like mushrooms.

The town at his feet, too, seemed nothing so much as a vague swarm of stars — or a single great column of smoke.



VI. Galaxy Station


Behind him, the weather-vane pillar had become a vague triangular marker. For a while it flickered on and off like a firefly — then grew steadily clearer, until at last it stood perfectly still on a deep steel-blue meadow of sky. It rose straight and keen from that field, like a plate of freshly tempered blue steel.


Then, from somewhere, a strange voice — "Galaxy Station, Galaxy Sta—"

The instant he thought he heard it, everything before his eyes blazed bright. It was as if the light of a billion firefly squid had been fossilized in a single stroke and sunk into the heavens — or as if some clerk at a diamond company, having hidden away stones too fine to sell cheap, had suddenly upended the whole cache and scattered them across the sky.

Giovanni rubbed his eyes again and again without meaning to.


When he came to himself, a small train had been running beneath him all along — clack-clack, clack-clack.

He really was sitting in the car of a night tram, a row of small yellow lamps above him, gazing out the window. The seats were upholstered in blue velvet and looked quite empty. On the opposite wall, varnished mouse-gray, two large brass buttons gleamed.


He noticed the child in the seat just ahead — tall, in a black coat that looked wet — leaning out the window. Something about the shoulders seemed familiar. The moment he thought so, he had to know who it was. He was about to lean out himself when the child suddenly pulled back and turned to look at him.

It was Campanella.


Giovanni was about to say, Campanella — you've been here the whole time? — when Campanella spoke first.

"Everyone ran and ran, but they were too late. Zanelli ran plenty too — he couldn't catch up."


Giovanni thought, (That's right — we set out together, the two of us.)

"Shall we wait for them somewhere?"

"Zanelli's already gone home. His father came to fetch him."

Campanella said it somehow with his face gone pale — as if in pain. Giovanni, too, felt a strange sense that he had forgotten something somewhere, and fell silent.


But Campanella, peering out the window, had already recovered completely. He spoke with vigor.

"Oh no — I forgot my canteen. And my sketchbook. But it doesn't matter. We'll reach Swan Station soon. I truly love watching swans. Even if they're flying far down the river, I'm sure I'll see them."

He was spinning a disc-shaped map round and round. Sure enough, a single railway line ran along the left bank of the Milky Way, traced in white, southward and southward again. The map was splendid — on a board black as night, every station and triangulation marker, every spring and forest, was picked out in beautiful lights of blue and orange and green. Giovanni felt he had seen that map somewhere before.

"Where did you get this? It's made of obsidian, isn't it," Giovanni said.

"I got it at Galaxy Station. Didn't you get one?"

"Did I pass through Galaxy Station? Where we are now — probably here."

Giovanni pointed just north of the station marked Swan.


"That's right. Oh — is that riverbank moonlit?"

He looked — and on the pale-glowing bank of the Milky Way, silver pampas grass rippled wind-to-wind across the whole surface, swaying, raising waves.

"It's not moonlight. It shines because it's the galaxy," Giovanni said.

He grew so joyful he nearly bounced from his seat. He tapped his feet, leaned far out the window, whistled the star-procession tune as high as he could, and stretched with all his might — trying to fix his eyes on the water of the Milky Way. At first he could not make it out clearly. But the more carefully he looked, the more that beautiful water seemed more transparent than glass, more than hydrogen — sometimes raising fine purple ripples, perhaps a trick of the eye, sometimes flashing like a rainbow, flowing on and on without a sound. Across the meadow, phosphorescent triangulation markers stood beautifully here and there. Distant ones small, near ones large; distant ones sharp in orange and yellow, near ones bluish and slightly dim — triangles, quadrilaterals, shapes like lightning or chains, lined up in every manner, lighting the whole field.

Giovanni's heart hammered. He shook his head hard — and truly, those triangulation markers glittering blue and orange across the beautiful meadow seemed to draw breath, flickering and trembling of their own accord.


"I've come all the way to the heavenly meadow," Giovanni said. "And this train isn't burning coal."

He stuck his left hand out the window and looked ahead as he spoke.

"Alcohol, probably. Or electric," Campanella said.


Clack-clack, clack-clack — the small beautiful train ran on and on through wind-swept sky-pampas, through the water of the Milky Way and the bluish faint glow of triangulation points, without end.


"Oh — asters are blooming. It's fully autumn now," Campanella said, pointing out the window.

Along the edge of the track, in the close-cropped grass, splendid purple asters bloomed — as if carved from moonstone.


"I could jump off, grab one, and jump back on — want to see me?"

Giovanni's chest leapt as he said it.

"Too late. We've already left it far behind—"

Before Campanella could finish, the next aster bloomed past them, full of light.


Then one after another, cups of aster with yellow bases sprang up like rain before their eyes. The row of triangulation markers smoked and burned, glowing brighter still.



VII. The Northern Cross and the Pleiocene Coast


"Will Mother forgive me?"

Suddenly Campanella spoke — as if forcing the words out, slightly halting, in a rush.


Giovanni thought, (Ah — that's right. My mother is there, near that distant orange triangulation marker that looks like a speck of dust. She's thinking of me right now.) He said nothing, dazed.


"I'd do anything if it would make Mother truly happy. But what, after all, would make her happiest?" Campanella seemed to be holding back tears with all his strength.

"Your mother hasn't done anything terrible!" Giovanni cried, startled.

"I don't know. But anyone who truly does good is happiest of all. So I think Mother will forgive me." Campanella looked as if he had made some real resolution.


Suddenly the car blazed white.

Water flowed soundlessly, formlessly, over a glittering riverbed of the galaxy — as if diamonds, dew on grass, and every splendor had been gathered there. In the middle of the current stood an island, pale and haloed. On its flat summit rose a white cross — splendid enough to wake the dead — as if cast from frozen arctic cloud, crowned with a keen golden nimbus, standing quietly, forever.


"Hallelujah. Hallelujah."

The voices rose from before and behind. Giovanni turned — every traveler in the car sat straight, kimono folds hanging evenly, black Bible pressed to the chest or crystal rosary in hand, fingers modestly clasped, praying toward the cross. Without thinking, the two boys stood up. Campanella's cheeks glowed beautifully, like the blush on a ripe apple.


The island and the cross gradually receded.


The opposite bank glowed bluish, puffing with mist. Now and then the pampas grass seemed to sway in the wind — its silver dimming as if someone had breathed on it. Aster flowers hiding and emerging from the grass looked like gentle fox-fires.


Only for a moment. A row of pampas screened the river from the train. Swan Island appeared twice more behind them — then grew distant, small as a picture, rustled away behind swaying grass, and vanished entirely.

Behind Giovanni sat a tall Catholic nun in black habit — when had she boarded? Her round green eyes were cast straight down, as if devoutly listening to words or a voice still arriving from that direction. The travelers quietly returned to their seats. The two boys, too, spoke softly in different languages, sharing a new feeling like sadness brimming in the chest.


"Swan Station's coming up soon."

"Yeah — we'll arrive at exactly eleven."


Already a green signal lamp and a vague white pillar flashed past the window. Then a dim light like glowing sulfur — the lamp before the switch — passed beneath them. The train gradually slowed. Soon a row of platform lamps appeared, beautifully regular, growing and spreading — and they came to a stop directly before the great clock at Swan Station.


On the fresh autumn clock face, two needles of blue-burned steel pointed clearly to eleven. Everyone disembarked at once. The car stood empty.

Beneath the clock: [Twenty-minute stop].


"Shall we get off and look around?" Giovanni said.

"Let's."

They bounded from their seats, flew through the door, and ran for the ticket gate — but there was only a single bright purplish lamp, and no one at all. No station master, no porter — not a shadow anywhere.


They came out into a small square before the station, ringed by ginkgo trees that looked like crystal work. From there a wide road ran straight into the blue light of the galaxy.


The passengers who had gotten off ahead were nowhere to be seen. Walking shoulder to shoulder down the white road, their shadows fell in every direction — like two pillars in a room with windows on all four sides, or like the spokes of two wheels multiplied again and again. Before long they reached the beautiful riverbank they had seen from the train.


Campanella pinched up a handful of the lovely sand, spread it on his palm, and made his fingers crackle as he spoke, dreamily,

"This sand is all crystal. Tiny fires burn inside."

"That's right," Giovanni answered vaguely, wondering where he had ever learned such a thing.


Every pebble on the riverbank was transparent — crystal, topaz, stones with wrinkled folds, corundum giving off a pale bluish mist from its edges. Giovanni ran to the shore and dipped his hands in the water. That strange Milky Way water was more transparent even than hydrogen. Yet it was certainly flowing: where their wrists entered the water seemed to float with a mercury sheen, and the ripples striking their wrists gave off a beautiful phosphorescence, flickering as if burning.


Looking upstream, beneath a cliff overgrown with pampas grass, white rock jutted flat along the river like an athletic field. Five or six small figures seemed to be digging something out or burying something — standing, bending, and now and then a tool flashing bright.


"Let's go see."

They shouted as one and ran that way. At the entrance to the white rock stood a smooth pottery sign: [Pleiocene Coast]. Along the opposite shore, thin iron railings had been planted here and there, and handsome wooden benches set out.


"Oh — something strange." Campanella stopped, puzzled, and picked up from the rock something like a black, elongated, pointed walnut.

"They're walnuts. Look — masses of them. They didn't wash downstream. They're embedded in the rock."

"They're huge. Twice the normal size. This one isn't hurt at all."

"Let's hurry over there. They're surely digging for something."


Walnuts jagged and black in hand, they approached again. On the left shore waves rolled in burning like gentle lightning. On the right cliff, pampas ears swayed across the face of it, as if sewn with silver and shells.


Drawing nearer, they saw a tall scholarly man in thick nearsighted glasses and long boots, feverishly writing in a notebook while directing three assistant-like men — now raising a pick-hammer, now using a scope — wholly absorbed in giving orders.

"Don't break that protrusion. Use the scope — the scope. Dig from a little farther back. Oh no, no — why such roughness!"

From the soft white rock, the bones of a great bluish beast had been half excavated, collapsed sideways as if crushed. Looking carefully, they saw that rocks bearing two-hoofed footprints had been neatly cut into squares — about ten of them — and numbered.


"Are you here to observe?" The university scholar glanced up, his glasses flashing. "There were plenty of walnuts, weren't there? Those are roughly a million and two hundred thousand years old — quite recent ones. This place was a coast after the Tertiary period, a million and two hundred thousand years ago. Shells come up from below. Where the river flows now, salt water ebbed and flowed just the same. This creature — it's called Bos — put down that pick, please, use a chisel carefully. Bos — ancestor of today's cattle. They were abundant long ago."

"Are you making a specimen?"

"No — we need it for proof. From our view this is a thick splendid stratum formed roughly a million and two hundred thousand years ago, and various evidence supports that — but from a different viewpoint, does it still look like such a stratum? Might it not look like wind, or water, or empty sky? Do you understand? — Oh, wait. You can't use a scope there either. Ribs should be buried just below." The scholar rushed off in a panic.


"It's time. Let's go," Campanella said, comparing his map and wristwatch.

"Ah — then we'll take our leave." Giovanni bowed politely to the scholar.

"Is that so? Well — goodbye." The scholar was already busy again, walking here and there, resuming his supervision.


The two ran across the white rock with all their might, desperate not to miss the train.

And truly — they ran like the wind. Their breath did not shorten; their knees did not grow hot.

If he could run like this, Giovanni thought, he could run across the whole world.

They passed the riverbank from before. The ticket-gate lamp grew steadily larger — and soon the two were back in their original seats, gazing out the window at where they had just been.



VIII. The Bird-Catcher


"May I sit here?"

A rustling but kindly adult voice sounded behind them.


It was a red-bearded man with a bent back, wearing a brown overcoat somewhat the worse for wear, his baggage — wrapped in white cloth and split in two — slung over both shoulders.


"Yes — please do." Giovanni dipped his shoulders in greeting.

The man smiled faintly through his beard and slowly stowed his bundles on the luggage rack. Giovanni felt a deep loneliness, a sadness he could not name, and stared silently at the clock straight ahead — when, far up the car, something like a glass flute sounded. The train was already moving quietly.

Campanella was studying the ceiling. A black beetle had settled on one of the lamps; its shadow loomed large above.

The red-bearded man watched Giovanni and Campanella with a nostalgic smile. The train gathered speed. Pampas grass and river flashed past the window, alternating light and dark.


The red-bearded man asked timidly,

"Where might you two be headed?"

"As far as it goes," Giovanni answered, slightly awkward.

"That's good. This train actually goes anywhere at all."

"Where are you going?" Campanella demanded suddenly — almost as if picking a fight — and Giovanni couldn't help laughing. The man in the seat opposite, pointed hat on his head and a large key at his waist, glanced over and laughed too. Campanella flushed and laughed as well. But the bird-catcher wasn't angry. His cheeks twitched as he replied,

"I get off just up ahead. Bird-catching's my trade."

"What birds?"

"Cranes and geese. Herons and swans too."

"Are there many cranes?"

"Plenty. They've been calling since a while back. Didn't you hear?"

"No."

"You can hear them now, can't you? Here — prick up your ears and listen."

They lifted their eyes and listened. Between the rumble of the train and the wind in the pampas grass came a sound like water bubbling — kororon, kororon.


"How do you catch cranes?"

"Cranes — or herons?"

"Herons," Giovanni said, thinking either would do.

"Those are easy. Herons, see — they're all just Milky Way sand that clumped together loosely. And they always come back to the river. You wait on the bank, and when the herons come down with their legs like this, before their feet touch the ground — you press them flat. Then the herons die peacefully, all together. After that it's simple. You just make pressed leaves of them."

"Pressed leaves of herons? Like specimens?"

"Not specimens. Everyone eats them, don't they?"

"How strange," Campanella said, tilting his head.

"Nothing strange about it. Nothing suspicious either. Here." The man stood, took a bundle from the rack, and unwrapped it swiftly. "Go on — look. Just caught these."

"Those really are herons!" they cried without thinking.

A dozen or so white bodies, glowing like that Northern Cross they had seen earlier, legs drawn up black and short, lined up slightly flattened like reliefs.


"Their eyes are closed." Campanella gently touched a heron's crescent-shaped white lid with his finger. The spear-like white feathers on its head were still perfectly in place.

"Yes — surely." The bird-catcher layered the furoshiki cloth, wrapped it round again, and tied it with string.

Who around here would eat herons? Giovanni wondered, and asked,

"Are herons tasty?"

"Yes — we get orders every day. But geese sell better. Geese have much finer markings, and they're far less trouble. Here." He unwrapped another bundle. Yellow and bluish-mottled geese glowed like lanterns, beaks aligned, slightly flattened — arranged just like the herons.


"These you can eat right away. How about it — have some." The bird-catcher gave a yellow goose's leg a light tug. It came away clean, as if made of chocolate.

"Go on — try a little." He tore one in two and handed it over.

Giovanni tasted a bite. (What — it's candy after all. Tastier than chocolate, even — but no geese fly like this. This man must be some confectioner from a field somewhere. And yet here I am eating his sweets while looking down on him — how pitiful.) He kept munching anyway.


"Have a little more." The bird-catcher produced another bundle. Giovanni wanted more — but said,

"Thank you — that's enough," and held back. The bird-catcher offered it instead to the key-man in the seat opposite.

"No — I couldn't take merchandise for nothing." The man removed his hat.

"Not at all, you're welcome. How's the migratory-bird trade this year?"

"Splendid, splendid. Day before yesterday, around the second watch — phones ringing from every direction, what's wrong with the lighthouse lamp, why's it going off-schedule — but it wasn't us. The migratory birds massed black as pitch, passing right before the beacon. Can't be helped. I told them — bring your complaints to that absurdly thin general in the rustling mantle, legs and mouth impossibly narrow, ha ha ha."


The pampas grass had ended. Light shot in from the field on the far side.


"Why are herons so much trouble?" Campanella asked — something he had wanted to ask for a while.

"Well, to eat a heron," the bird-catcher said, turning back toward them, "you've got to hang it in the Milky Way's water-light for ten days — or else bury it in sand three or four days. That drives all the mercury off, and then it's fit to eat."

"These aren't birds at all. They're just candy." Campanella asked boldly — apparently thinking the same thing Giovanni had. The bird-catcher looked terribly flustered.

"That's right — got to get off here." He stood, gathered his baggage — and was already gone.


"Where did he go?" The two looked at each other. The lighthouse keeper grinned, stretched up slightly, and peered out their side window. They looked too.

There stood the bird-catcher, on a riverside meadow glowing yellow and bluish with beautiful phosphorescence — serious face, both arms spread, gazing fixedly at the sky.


"There he is. How peculiar. Surely catching birds again. I hope they come down before the train moves on—"

The moment he said it, from the vast bellflower-colored sky, herons came crying gyaagya, descending thick as snowfall — just as they had seen before. The bird-catcher looked thoroughly pleased, as if this were exactly the order. He stood with both legs spread at a crisp sixty degrees and, with both hands, pressed the drawn-up black legs of each descending heron, one after another, into a cloth bag.

The herons glowed in the bag like fireflies — blue, flickering on and off — then gradually dulled to a vague white and closed their eyes.

More birds, though, landed safely on the Milky Way sand than were caught. Watching them: the moment their feet touched sand they shrank and flattened as if snow were melting, then spread across the sand and gravel like molten copper from a furnace. For a while the bird-shape clung to the ground — but after two or three cycles of brightening and dimming, it was altogether the same color as its surroundings.


After about twenty birds were in the bag, the catcher suddenly raised both hands in the pose of a soldier struck by a bullet and dying — and the bird-catcher's form was gone. Instead—

"Ah — what a relief. Nothing better than earning just enough to suit the body."

A familiar voice spoke beside Giovanni. The bird-catcher was already there, neatly arranging the herons he had caught, stacking them one by one.


"How did you get here from over there all at once?" Giovanni asked, feeling something at once ordinary and not ordinary, familiar and strange.

"How? I came because I wanted to. Where are you two from, anyway?"

Giovanni was about to answer — but he couldn't think, no matter how he tried, where they had come from at all. Campanella's cheeks flushed red as he seemed to be trying to remember something.


"Ah — from far away." The bird-catcher nodded casually, as if he understood perfectly.



IX. Giovanni's Ticket


"We're at the end of the Swan district now. Look — that's the famous Albireo Observatory."


Out the window, in the middle of the Milky Way — blazing like a sky full of fireworks — stood four large black buildings. On the flat roof of one, two great transparent spheres, sapphire and topaz, revolved quietly in a ring — splendid enough to wake the dead.

The yellow sphere drifted away; the small blue one advanced. Before long their edges overlapped, forming the shape of a beautiful green biconvex lens. The center swelled outward; the blue came fully to the fore — a green heart with a bright yellow ring. Then it slid sideways, reversed the lens shape, drew apart at last: the sapphire circled away, the yellow advanced, and everything returned exactly as before.

Surrounded by the Milky Way's formless, soundless water, the black observatory lay stretched out quietly — as if asleep.


"That instrument measures the speed of the water. The water too—" the bird-catcher began — when—

"Your tickets, please."

A tall conductor in a red cap had been standing straight beside their seats without anyone noticing. The bird-catcher silently produced a small slip of paper from his breast. The conductor glanced at it, looked away at once, and — (And yours?) — wiggling his fingers, extended his hand toward Giovanni and the others.


"Well—" Giovanni fidgeted, at a loss.

Campanella, as if it were nothing at all, produced a small mouse-gray ticket. Giovanni was completely flustered. Perhaps it was in his coat pocket — he reached in and touched something like a large folded paper. Could such a thing have been there? He pulled it out in a hurry.

It was green paper, folded in four, about the size of a postcard. The conductor's hand was still out — anything would do, just hand it over — so Giovanni passed it over. The conductor stood straight, opened it carefully, and read. He straightened the buttons on his coat as he read; the lighthouse keeper peered up at it keenly from below. Giovanni felt certain it was some kind of certificate. His chest grew warm.


"Did you bring this from third-dimensional space?" the conductor asked.

"I don't really know." Giovanni looked up and chuckled — reassured now that things were all right.


"Very well. You will arrive at the Southern Cross around the third hour, next stop." The conductor returned the paper and went on his way.


Campanella leaned in urgently, as if he had been waiting to see what the slip was. Giovanni wanted badly to look too. But it was printed with some ten odd characters amid an all-over black arabesque pattern — and the longer he stared, the more he felt drawn into it.

The bird-catcher glanced over from the side and said, alarmed,

"Oh — this is something. A real ticket to heaven. Not just heaven — a pass good anywhere you please. No wonder you can ride this incomplete, illusory fourth-edition Galactic Railroad to the ends of the earth. You two are something else."


"I don't really know." Giovanni flushed, folded it again, and tucked it away.

Awkward, he and Campanella turned back to the window — though he was dimly aware of the bird-catcher glancing their way now and then, as if to say, Well now, that's something.


"Eagle Station's coming up soon," Campanella said, comparing the map with three small bluish triangulation markers on the opposite bank.


For no reason Giovanni could name, he was suddenly overcome with pity for the bird-catcher beside him. He thought of how the man had rejoiced after catching herons, how he wrapped them in white cloth, how he had peeked at someone else's ticket in amazement and praised it — and considering each thing one by one, he felt he would give this stranger anything he had, food or otherwise. If it would bring the man true happiness, Giovanni would stand on that glowing heavenly riverbank and catch birds for a hundred years. He could not stay silent any longer. He almost asked, What is it you truly want? — but that seemed too blunt; he was still wondering what to do when he turned around.

The bird-catcher was already gone. No white bundles on the luggage rack either. Perhaps he was outside, bracing his legs and looking up at the sky, getting ready to catch herons — Giovanni looked quickly — but outside was only beautiful sand and waves of white pampas grass. No broad back, no pointed hat.


"Where did he go?" Campanella murmured, dazed.


"Where did he go? Where will we ever meet again? I should have said something to him — just a little."

"Ah — I was thinking the same."


"I felt like he was in the way. That's why it hurts so much." Giovanni had never felt such a strange mood before — never said anything like this, he thought.


"Somehow I smell apples. Maybe it's because I was just thinking about apples." Campanella looked around, puzzled.

"You're right — it really is apples. And wild roses too." Giovanni looked about, but the scents seemed to drift in through the window. Wild roses shouldn't bloom in autumn, he thought.


Then, suddenly, a boy of about six stood there — glossy black hair, red jacket unbuttoned, face terribly frightened, trembling, barefoot. Beside him a tall young man in a neat black Western suit held the boy's hand firmly, standing in a posture like a keyaki tree blown by the wind.


"Oh — where are we? How beautiful." Behind the young man stood a girl of about twelve — brown eyes, charming — in a black coat, clinging to his arm and gazing wonderingly out the window.


"Ah — this is Lancashire. No — Connecticut. No — ah, we've come to the sky. We're going to heaven. Look — that marker is a heavenly sign. There's nothing to fear anymore. We are summoned by God."

The man in black spoke to the girl, shining with joy. Yet somehow deep wrinkles lined his forehead; he looked terribly weary. With a forced smile he seated the boy beside Giovanni — then gently indicated the seat next to Campanella for the girl. The girl sat obediently, hands neatly folded.


"I'm going to big sister's house," the boy said, his face strange, to the young man who had just sat across from the lighthouse keeper. The young man could say nothing; his face was sorrowful as he stared at the boy's curled, damp head. The girl suddenly covered her face with both hands and sobbed.


"Father and sister will hear — there's still work to do, but they'll come soon. Think how long Mother has been waiting. My dear Tadashi — what song is he singing now? On a snowy morning, is he spinning hand in hand with the others through the hazel thicket? She's truly been waiting, truly worried — so let's hurry and go see Mother."

"Yeah — but I wish I hadn't gotten on the ship."

"Yes — but look at the sky. That splendid river — there, where in summer you rested singing Twinkle, Twinkle, Little Star, always vaguely white through the window. That's the place. See how beautifully it glows."

The sister, still crying, wiped her eyes with a handkerchief and looked out. The young man spoke again softly to the siblings, as if teaching them.

"We have nothing to grieve for anymore. We're traveling to such a fine place — soon we'll reach God. There it's truly bright, fragrant, full of splendid people. And those who boarded the boat in our place will surely all be saved — they'll go home to the fathers and mothers and houses that wait for them, anxious. Come — it's almost time. Cheer up and sing happily along the way."

He stroked the boy's damp black hair, comforting them all — and gradually his own face began to brighten.


"Where have you come from? What happened?" the lighthouse keeper asked the young man — as if he had finally understood a little. The young man laughed faintly.

"No — our ship struck an iceberg and sank. Our father had urgent business and returned to our country a month early; we followed two months later. I was at university, hired as a tutor. Then on the twelfth day — today, or perhaps yesterday — the ship struck an iceberg and listed at once, nearly sinking. The moon was somewhere vague; the fog was terribly thick. But the lifeboats on the port side — half of them were already useless — so not everyone could board. The ship was sinking fast. I cried out desperately — please, put the little ones aboard. Those nearby made way at once, and prayed for the children. But between us and the boats there were still so many small children, parents — I hadn't the courage to push them aside. Still, I thought it my duty to save these children before me, and tried to push through those in front. Yet I also thought that going to God together as we were might truly be these children's happiness. And the sin of defying God — I alone would bear it; I had to save them. But watching, I could not. Only the children were let into the boat — mothers kissing wildly, fathers standing straight, holding back their grief — it was gut-wrenching. The ship sank steadily. I made up my mind, held these two, and waited together to float when it went under. Someone threw a life buoy — it slipped away into the distance. I opened the deck grating with all my strength; the three of us clung to it. A hymn rose from somewhere — and at once everyone sang it together in every language. Then suddenly a great sound — and we fell into the water. I thought we were in the whirlpool, held them tight — then grew dazed — and here we were. Their mother passed away the year before last. The boat was surely saved — skilled sailors were rowing, after all, and had quickly pulled away from the ship."


From here and there came small sighs and prayers. Giovanni and Campanella remembered many things they had forgotten; their eyes grew hot.


(Ah — wasn't that great sea called the Pacific? At the northern edge where icebergs drift, aboard a small ship, fighting wind and freezing tide and bitter cold — someone is working with all his might. I pity that person — I feel I owe him something. What can I possibly do for his sake?)

Giovanni hung his head, utterly cast down.


"I don't know what coincidence means. But truly — any hardship, if it comes while walking the just path, whether climbing or descending a pass, is each a step nearer true happiness." The lighthouse keeper was consoling him.

"Ah — yes. All sorrows are God's will, leading to the greatest happiness," the young man answered, as if in prayer.


The brother and sister were exhausted now, each slumped in a seat, asleep. The bare feet from before wore white soft shoes.


Clack-clack, clack-clack — the train advanced along the bank of a river glittering with phosphorescence. Through the window on the far side, the field was like a magic lantern. Hundreds, thousands of triangulation markers large and small — on the larger ones, survey flags dotted with red. At the field's edge they gathered in masses, a pale bluish mist; from there, or from farther off, vague signal-smoke of various shapes rose and fell against the beautiful bellflower-colored sky. Truly that transparent, lovely wind was full of the scent of roses.


"How do you like it? You've never seen apples like these." The lighthouse keeper across the way was cradling a large apple on his knees with both hands — beautifully colored in gold and crimson, lest it fall.


"My — where did those come from? How splendid. Do apples like these grow around here?" The young man forgot himself, squinting and craning his neck as he admired the heap of apples in the lighthouse keeper's hands.


"No — please, take some. Do take one." The young man picked one up and glanced toward Giovanni and the others. "Here — young gentlemen. How about it? Please help yourselves." Giovanni was slightly annoyed at being called young gentleman and said nothing — but Campanella said, "Thank you." The young man handed them one each himself, so Giovanni stood and thanked him too.


At last the lighthouse keeper's arms were free. He placed an apple gently on each sleeping sibling's knee.


"Thank you so much. Where do they grow? Apples this fine—" the young man said, studying one closely.

"Agriculture is practiced in these parts, of course — but it's mostly arranged so good things grow more or less on their own. It doesn't break your back. Generally you need only sow the seed you want, and things grow abundantly by themselves. Rice, too — like in the Pacific — has no husk, is ten times larger, and smells wonderful. But where you come from, there's no agriculture anymore. Apples, sweets — not a speck of chaff. Each one ripens to its own slight perfection and scatters from the hair follicle, so to speak."


Suddenly the boy opened his eyes wide.

"Ah — I was dreaming of Mother. She was in a fine place with shelves and books, looking at me, holding out her hands, smiling and smiling. I said, Mother, shall I pick apples for you? — and I woke up. Oh — we're in the train from before."


"There's the apple. This gentleman gave it to us," the young man said.

"Thank you, sir. Oh — Kaoru, sister's still asleep. I'll wake her. Sister — look, we got apples. Wake up." The sister laughed, opened her eyes, shaded them with both hands — then saw the apple. The boy was already eating his, as if it were pie. Even the peel, once stripped, curled like a corkscrew and, before it hit the floor, glowed gray and evaporated in an instant.


The two boys tucked their apples carefully into their pockets.


Downstream on the opposite bank stood a great blue-green wood. Its branches were heavy with round fruit, ripe and glowing red. In the wood's center rose a tall triangulation marker — and from within the forest came indescribably beautiful tones, melting and soaking into the wind, mingled with orchestra bells and a xylophone.


The young man shuddered, then seemed to shake it off.


Listening in silence to that music, Giovanni felt a bright meadow or carpet spread yellow and pale green across the scene — and dew like white wax seemed to brush the face of the sun.


"Oh — that crow!" cried the girl called Kaoru, beside Campanella.

"Not crows. They're all magpies," Campanella called back casually, as if scolding — and Giovanni laughed despite himself. The girl looked embarrassed. Truly, above the bluish glow of the riverbank, black birds stood in dense ranks, still, catching the river's faint light.


"Magpies — you can tell by the feathers sticking straight out behind the head," the young man said, mediating.


The triangulation marker in the blue wood had come squarely before the train. Then, from far behind the car, came the melody of a familiar hymn — sung, it seemed, by a considerable chorus. The young man's face went pale; he half rose as if to go that way at once, then thought better of it and sat down again. Kaoru pressed a handkerchief to her face. Even Giovanni's nose felt strange. Yet before long the hymn was taken up by everyone, growing steadily clearer and stronger — and Giovanni and Campanella found themselves singing along.


The blue olive wood gleamed faintly on the far side of the Milky Way and gradually fell behind. The strange instrument-sounds drifting from there were worn away by the train's rumble and the wind, fading to nothing.

"Oh — peacocks!"

"Yes — there were lots."

Giovanni watched above the wood — now small as a green button — as peacocks flickered bluish light, spreading and folding their wings.


"That's right — we heard peacock voices earlier," Campanella said to Kaoru.

"Yes — thirty or so, surely. What sounded like harps was all peacocks," the girl answered.

Giovanni felt a sadness he could not name. He almost said, with a frightened face, "Campanella — let's jump off here and play" —

(I'm going to leave, Campanella. I've never even seen a whale.)

Giovanni bit his lip hard and stared out the window, unbearably restless. Dolphins were no longer visible; the river had split in two. In the center of a dark island stood a high scaffold, and atop it a man in loose clothes and a red cap, signaling to the sky with red and blue flags. While Giovanni watched, the man waved the red flag steadily — then suddenly lowered it behind him and raised the blue high, shaking it fiercely like an orchestra conductor. A sound like rain burst through the air; several dark masses flew like cannonballs toward the far side of the river. Giovanni leaned halfway out the window and looked up. Beneath a beautiful, beautiful bellflower sky, tens of thousands of small birds passed in countless flocks, crying busily.

"Birds are flying," Giovanni said out the window.

"Look," Campanella said, gazing up too. Just then the man on the scaffold raised his red flag and waved it madly. The flock stopped passing at once — and at the same moment a crushed sound rose downstream, followed by a long ringing silence. Then the red-capped signalman waved his blue flag and shouted — clearly audible:

"Now cross, migratory birds — now cross, migratory birds!"

With that, another flock of tens of thousands shot straight across the sky. The girl leaned out the window between the two boys, cheeks shining, and looked up.

"My — so many birds. How beautiful the sky is." She spoke to Giovanni — but he thought her cheeky and annoying, said nothing, and only pursed his lips, gazing upward. The girl sighed softly and returned to her seat. Campanella withdrew his face from the window with pity and studied his map.


"Is that man teaching the birds?" the girl asked Campanella quietly.

"He's signaling to the migratory birds. Probably because smoke is rising somewhere," Campanella answered, somewhat uncertain.

The car fell silent. Giovanni wanted to pull his head in — but it was painful to show his face in the light, so he endured, standing and whistling.


(Why am I so sad? I have to keep my heart cleaner, bigger. Far across that shore I can see small blue fires like smoke. They're truly quiet and cold. I'll look at them well and calm my heart.)

Giovanni pressed his fever-hot head with both hands and looked that way.

(Ah — is there really no one who'll go with me forever and ever? Even Campanella talks so cheerfully with that girl. I'm truly miserable.)

Giovanni's eyes filled with tears again. The Milky Way looked only vaguely white, as if it had gone far away.


The train gradually left the river and passed along the clifftop. The opposite bank, too, showed black cliffs growing steadily higher downstream. Giovanni caught a glimpse of a great corn plant — leaves curled in spirals, and beneath them beautiful green sheaths with red hairs, pearl-like fruit peeking through. More and more appeared in rows between cliff and track. Giovanni pulled his head from the window and looked out the other side — and from horizon to horizon across the heavenly meadow, great corn plants stood almost wall to wall, rustling in the wind. Dew clung to the tips of their splendid curled leaves like diamonds that had drunk sunlight all day, burning and glittering red and green.

"That's corn," Campanella said to Giovanni — but Giovanni's spirits would not lift. He only stared bluntly at the meadow and answered, "I suppose so."

The train gradually slowed, passed several signals and switch lamps, and stopped at a small station.


The bluish clock on the platform face showed the second hour precisely. Its pendulum ticked on — windless, train motionless — marking time correctly in the quiet quiet meadow.


And around then the train began to ring like the New World Symphony. Inside the car, the tall young man in black and everyone else were dreaming gentle dreams.

(Why can't I be happier in such a quiet place? Why am I so terribly alone? And Campanella — it's too much. Riding on the train with me and talking only to that girl. I'm truly miserable.)

Giovanni covered half his face with both hands again and stared out the opposite window. A transparent, glass-like flute sounded; the train moved quietly. Campanella, too, blew the star-procession tune — lonely and alone.


"Yes, yes — this area is a terrible plateau." From the rear came a crisp voice, as if someone elderly had just woken. "You have to bore holes two feet deep with a pole before you can plant corn."

"Is that so. It's quite a way to the river, isn't it?"

"Yes, yes — two to six thousand feet to the river. It's become a dreadful canyon."

Colorado plateau — isn't this the Colorado plateau? Giovanni thought involuntarily.

Across the way, the elder sister held her little sister against her chest, sleeping, black eyes cast raptly into the distance, lost in thought. Campanella still whistled alone, forlorn. The second girl — Kaoru — had a face like an apple wrapped in silk, and was looking Giovanni's way.

Suddenly the corn vanished and a vast black field opened wide. The New World Symphony swelled clearly from the horizon's edge — and across that black field an Indian came chasing the train at a run, feathers of a white bird on his head, many stones hung on arms and chest, nocking an arrow to a small bow.

"Oh — an Indian! An Indian! Sister, look!" The young man in black woke too. Giovanni and Campanella stood up.

"He's running — oh, he's running! He must be chasing us!"

"No — he's not chasing the train. He's hunting or dancing." The young man stood with hands in his pockets, as if he had forgotten where he was.


Truly the Indian seemed half to be dancing. Even if he ran at full speed, his footwork looked economical — as if he could go harder still. Suddenly the white feathers tipped forward; the Indian stopped dead and swiftly drew his bow to the sky. A crane wavered down from above and fell into the Indian's great outspread hands as he ran on. The Indian stood, laughing with joy — then, crane in hand, looked this way; but his shadow was already shrinking distant. Telegraph insulators glittered in a row, two of them flashed — and it was corn forest again.

Through the window on this side, the train truly ran along a high high cliff; far below in the valley the river flowed wide and bright.


"Yes — from here it's downhill. After all, we're going straight down to that water surface in one go — no easy matter. With this grade the train never comes from the opposite direction. See — it's getting faster already." The elderly-sounding voice spoke again.


Down, down, down, down went the train. When the track crossed the cliff's edge, the river gleamed brightly below. Giovanni's spirits grew steadily lighter. As the train passed a small hut with a solitary child standing before it, looking this way, he cried out without thinking.


Down, down, down, down the train ran. Everyone in the car clung to their seats, half tilted backward. Giovanni laughed with Campanella despite himself. The Milky Way now flowed fiercely just beside the train, flashing now and then. Pale-red anemones bloomed here and there along the bank. At last the train seemed to settle and ran slowly.


On the near and far shores stood pennants marked with a star and a pickaxe.

"What kind of flag is that?" Giovanni said at last.

"Who knows — it's not on the map. There's an iron boat laid up."

"Oh."

"Isn't that where they're building a bridge?" the girl said.

"Ah — those are engineer corps flags. Bridge-building drill. But you can't see any soldiers."

Just then, near the opposite bank a little downstream, the invisible Milky Way water flashed and leaped high like a pillar — a tremendous boom. "Blasting — blasting!" Campanella danced with excitement.

The pillar of water vanished. Great salmon and trout flashed white bellies in the air, thrown up in arcs, and fell back into the water. Giovanni felt light enough to jump. "Sky engineer corps! Look — trout hurled up like this! I've never had such a delightful journey. Isn't it fine?"

"Those trout — seen up close they'd be about this big. Plenty of fish in this water."

"Are there little fish too?" the girl said, drawn into the conversation.

"There must be. If there are big ones there must be small ones. But they're far away — we can't see the small ones now." Giovanni's mood had fully recovered; he answered the girl with an amused laugh.


"That's surely the shrine of the Twin Stars!" the boy cried suddenly, pointing out the window.


On a low hill to the right stood two small shrines side by side, as if carved from crystal.


"What are the Twin Stars?"

"I've heard from Mother many times. They're proper little crystal shrines, standing in a pair — it must be them."

"Tell us. What did the Twin Stars do?"

"I know too. The Twin Stars went out to play in the meadow and fought with a crow—"

"That's not it. You see, on the bank of the Milky Way, Mother told me the story—"

"And then a comet came going gee-gee-foo gee-gi-foo—"

"No, brother, that's not it. That's a different one."

"Then is he playing the flute there now?"

"He's gone to the sea now."

"You mustn't. He's already come up from the sea."

"Yes, yes — I know, I'll tell the story."


The opposite bank suddenly turned red. Poplar trees and the like showed black in silhouette; the invisible Milky Way waves flashed red now and then like needles. Truly on the meadow of the far bank a great crimson fire burned; its black smoke rose high enough to scorch the cold bellflower-colored sky. More red than ruby, more transparent than lithium — drunk with beauty — the fire burned.

"What kind of fire is that? What do you burn to get a fire that red?" Giovanni said.

"Scorpion fire," Campanella answered again, map in hand, craning his neck.

"Oh — if it's scorpion fire, I know about that."

"What is scorpion fire?" Giovanni asked.

"A scorpion burned to death. That fire still burns — Father told me many times."

"A scorpion's an insect, right?"

"Yes — a scorpion's an insect. But a good one."

"Scorpions aren't good insects. I saw one in alcohol at the museum. There's a hook on its tail — the teacher said you die if it stings you."

"That's right. But it's a good insect — Father said so. Long ago on the plain of Baldra there was a scorpion that killed little insects and such to eat and live. One day a weasel found it and was about to eat it. The scorpion ran for its life — ran and ran — but at last the weasel was about to pin it down. Then suddenly a well appeared before it and it fell in. It couldn't climb out and began to drown. Then the scorpion prayed like this—

Ah — I don't know how many lives I've taken. And when I was about to be taken by the weasel I fled with all my might — yet in the end it came to this. Ah — nothing can be relied on. Why didn't I give my body quietly to the weasel? Then the weasel would have lived another day. Please, God — look upon my heart. Don't let me throw away my life so vainly — next time, use my body truly for everyone's happiness.

— and then one day the scorpion saw its own body become a crimson beautiful fire, burning to light the darkness of night. It still burns — Father said. That fire is truly it."

"That's right. Look — those triangulation markers are lined up exactly in the shape of a scorpion."

Giovanni saw plainly that beyond the great fire, three triangulation markers stood like a scorpion's arms — and on this side five markers lined up like its tail and hook. And truly that crimson beautiful scorpion fire burned on and on, brightly, silently.


As the fire gradually fell behind, everyone heard indescribably festive music, scents like grass and flowers, whistles, and the murmur of voices. It felt as if a town were near — as if a festival were about to begin.


"Centaurus — scatter the dew!"

The boy beside Giovanni — asleep until now — cried out, gazing out the opposite window.


Ah — there stood blue hinoki or maple trees like Christmas trees, hung with masses of tiny bulbs — as if a thousand fireflies had gathered.


"Ah — that's right, tonight's the Centaur festival."

"Ah — this is Centaurus village," Campanella said at once.

"I'd never lose at throwing a ball," the boy said proudly.

"Southern Cross is coming soon. Please get ready to get off," the young man told everyone.

"I'm riding the train a little longer too," the boy said. The girl beside Campanella stood hurriedly and began to prepare — yet she seemed not to want to part from Giovanni and the others.

"You have to get off here." The young man said it firmly, lips pressed together, looking down at the boy.

"I don't want to. I want to ride the train a little longer first," Giovanni said, unable to hold back. "Let's ride together — we have tickets that go anywhere."

"But we have to get off here too. This is where we go to heaven," the girl said sadly.

"We don't have to go to heaven. My teacher said we have to build a place right here that's even better than heaven."

"But Mother is going, and God commands it."

"Your God — a false god."

"Your God is the false one."

"No it's not."

"What kind of God is your God?" the young man asked, smiling.

"I don't really know — but not a false one. The one true God."

"The true God is of course only one."

"Ah — not a false one. The one true, true God."

"So you see. I pray that you will soon stand before that true God — together with us." The young man clasped his hands modestly. The girl did exactly the same. Everyone truly seemed sorry to part; their faces looked slightly pale. Giovanni nearly burst into tears.


"Are you ready? Southern Cross is almost here."

Ah — it was at that moment.

Far downstream on the invisible Milky Way, a cross glittering in every light — blue, orange, and all the rest — stood in the river as if it were a single tree. Above it a bluish cloud ringed it like a nimbus. The car stirred. Everyone stood straight and began to pray, as at the Northern Cross before. Joy like children leaping at gourds — and deep, inexpressible sighs — sounded here and there. The cross came squarely before the window; the bluish cloud-ring, like apple flesh, wound slowly, slowly round.


"Hallelujah. Hallelujah."

Bright and joyful voices rang. From far cold sky — from transparent, indescribably fresh trumpet-sound — they heard. Through many signals and lamps the train gradually slowed, drew toward the cross, and came to a full stop directly opposite it.

"Now — time to get off." The young man took the boy's hand. The sisters straightened each other's collars and shoulders and walked toward the exit.

"Well — goodbye." The girl turned back to the two of them.

"Goodbye." Giovanni held back tears he nearly shed and answered bluntly, as if angry.

The girl's eyes widened, terribly pained — looked back once more — then went out in silence. More than half the car stood empty. Suddenly it was hollow and lonely; wind blew in full.


Watching, Giovanni saw everyone form a modest line and kneel on the shore of the Milky Way before that cross. A divine white figure stretched out hands and came toward them across the invisible water — the two boys saw. But already the glass whistle sounded; the train moved — and silver mist flowed up from downstream, hiding everything. Only many walnut trees glittered through the leaves, and a golden-nimbus electric squirrel peeked out with a cute face from within the mist.


Then the mist cleared somewhat. A road seemed to lead somewhere — a row of small lamps along it, following the track for a while. When the two passed before those lamps, the tiny bean-colored flames went out as if in greeting, and lit again when they had passed.


Looking back, the cross had grown quite small — truly as if it might hang on one's chest. Whether the girl and the young people still knelt on the white shore before it, or had gone to that heaven whose direction no one knew — Giovanni could not tell, dazed.


Giovanni drew a deep breath. "Ah—"

"Campanella — we're alone again, the two of us. Let's go together forever and ever. I'm truly ready to burn my body a hundred times for everyone's happiness — like that scorpion."

"Yes — me too." Beautiful tears rose in Campanella's eyes.

"But what is true happiness?" Giovanni said.

"I don't know," Campanella murmured.


"Let's do our best — together." Giovanni said it with a full breath, as if new strength welled up in his chest.


"Oh — coal sacks over there. Holes in the sky." Campanella pointed to a spot on the Milky Way, leaning slightly away.

Giovanni looked — and started violently. A great black hole gaped open in one place on the Milky Way. However deep the bottom, whatever lay within — rub his eyes as he might, he saw nothing; only his eyes ached sharply.

"I'm not afraid of such great darkness anymore. I'll search for everyone's true happiness. Let's go on together — forever and ever."

"Ah — surely we will. Ah — how beautiful that meadow is. Everyone's gathered. That's the true heaven — ah, my mother is there!" Campanella cried suddenly, pointing at a beautiful meadow visible far through the window.


Giovanni looked too — but it was only vaguely white and hazy; he could not see it as Campanella did. An indescribable loneliness came over him; he gazed blankly that way — when on the opposite bank two telegraph poles stood with red cross-arms, as if linking arms from both sides.

"Campanella — let's go together."

Giovanni said it and turned around — but Campanella's form was no longer in the seat where he had been sitting. Giovanni shot up as if struck by a bullet. He leaned halfway out the window where no one could hear, beat his chest with all his strength and shouted — then sobbed until his throat was full. Everything seemed to go black at once.


Giovanni opened his eyes.

He had fallen asleep exhausted in the grass on the original hill. His chest felt strangely hot; cold tears ran down his cheeks.


Giovanni sprang up like a spring.

Below, the town was strung with lights just as before — yet the glow seemed somehow riper than earlier. The Milky Way he had walked in his dream hung white and hazy as before, especially misty above the black southern horizon. To its right the red star of Scorpio glittered beautifully. The sky as a whole seemed hardly to have shifted at all.


Giovanni ran down the hill at once. His mother, still waiting without supper, filled his chest. He passed through the black pine grove, rounded the pale pasture fence, and came again through the entrance to the dark cowshed. Someone had evidently just returned — a cart stood there that hadn't been before, two barrels loaded on it.


"Good evening!" Giovanni called.

"Yes." A man in thick white trousers came out at once and stood.

"What can I do for you?"

"The milk didn't come to our house today."

"Ah — I'm sorry." The man went straight inside and returned with a bottle of milk. "Truly, I'm sorry. Today I was careless — out all day — and left the bamboo gate open. The lead cow went straight to her mother and drank about half—" He laughed.

"I see. Well, I'll take it."

"Yes — thank you very much."

"No — it's fine." Giovanni cupped the still-warm bottle in both palms and left through the pasture gate.


He passed through the tree-lined town to the main road, walked a while — and came to a crossroads. At the edge of the road to the right, the great bridge-frame over the river stood vaguely against the night sky — the same river where Campanella and the others had gone to float their gourd lanterns.


But at the crossroads and before the shops, women gathered in clusters of seven or eight, whispering as they looked toward the bridge. The bridge itself blazed with many lights.


Giovanni's chest went cold without warning. He turned suddenly to those nearby.

"Did something happen?" he asked — almost shouting.

"A child fell in the water," someone said — and they all looked at Giovanni at once.

Giovanni ran toward the bridge as if in a trance. The bridge was packed with people; he couldn't see the river. A policeman in white stood among them.

Giovanni flew down from the bridge's edge to the wide riverbank below.


Along the water's edge many lanterns bobbed busily up and down. On the dark opposite bank seven or eight fires moved. Through the middle flowed the river — no gourd lanterns now — gray and quiet, murmuring faintly.


At the downstream end of the riverbank, where it jutted out like a sandbar, a crowd stood black and distinct. Giovanni ran that way — and suddenly met Marso, who had been with Campanella earlier. Marso ran up to him.

"Giovanni — Campanella went into the river."

"Why? When?"

"Zanelli — he was on a boat, trying to push a gourd lantern downstream. The boat rocked and he fell in. Campanella jumped in right away. He pushed Zanelli back toward the boat. Zanelli grabbed the gunwale. But after that — Campanella's gone."

"Everyone's searching?"

"Yeah — everyone came at once. Campanella's father too. But they can't find him. Zanelli was taken home."

Giovanni went toward the crowd. Campanella's father stood there — black suit, sharp bluish chin — surrounded by students and townspeople, staring fixedly at the watch in his right hand.


Everyone stared at the river. No one spoke a word. Giovanni's legs trembled and trembled. Acetylene lamps for night fishing hurried back and forth; the black river water rippled in small waves, flashing.


Downstream the river reflected the Milky Way hugely — as if it were sky itself, without water.


Giovanni felt certain Campanella was already at the edge of that galaxy — and could not shake the feeling.


Yet everyone still seemed to feel — as if from between the waves —

"I swam plenty" — Campanella might emerge saying — or Campanella might have reached some unknown sandbar and stood waiting for someone to come.

But suddenly Campanella's father said firmly,

"It's no use. Forty-five minutes have passed since he fell."

Giovanni started forward and stood before the doctor, wanting to say — I know where Campanella went, I was walking with Campanella — but his throat closed; he could say nothing. The doctor, as if Giovanni had come to greet him, studied him a while —

"You're Giovanni, aren't you. Thank you for this evening," he said politely.

Giovanni said nothing — only bowed.

"Has your father returned yet?" the doctor asked again, gripping his watch tightly.

"No." Giovanni shook his head faintly.

"I wonder what's wrong. I had a very cheerful letter from him the day before yesterday — he should have arrived today by now. The ship must be delayed. Giovanni — come play at our house tomorrow after school, with everyone."

Saying so, the doctor sent his gaze fixedly downstream — toward where the Milky Way lay reflected full across the water.

Giovanni's chest was too full of everything to speak. He left the doctor's side, wanting to hurry home with the milk for his mother and tell her their father was returning — and ran at full speed from the riverbank toward the town.
